Iris

Iris is a fictional app that allows users to monitor their menstrual cycle, hormones, and skincare.

Oct - Dec 2021

Summary

Iris is an app that lets users track their skincare and menstrual cycle together, so they can learn how each phase of their cycle affects their skin. My team designed this prototype using Lean UX.

As a co-designer, my role centered on designing the homepage and the app's information pages, and facilitating interviews. Each of us moderated at least one interview. The team consisted of Stephanie Meneses (Team Leader), Jacob Nguyen, Annie Parimango, and Alex Manuel.

Overview

The app store is full of services for tracking the menstrual cycle, and there are a few for tracking skincare. But current products focus on external skin stressors and fail to address internal ones like mood and diet. They also ignore how the menstrual cycle affects skin, such as hormone fluctuations that cause irregularities like acne. There are skincare apps, and there are cycle-tracking apps, but nothing brings the two together.

Our solution was Iris: a tracking app that lets users monitor their skincare and menstrual cycle in one place, and learn how each phase of their cycle affects their skin.


Mission: Help users better understand their menstrual cycle and its relationship with their skin.


We designed this prototype using Lean UX. Traditional UX design centers on deliverables and usability; it asks "what are we making?" Lean UX instead asks "are we making the right thing?" and focuses designers on changes that improve the product. Lean UX is a cycle of:

  • Outcomes, assumptions, hypothesis
  • Collaborative design
  • Creating an MVP
  • Research and learning
Lean UX is often used by product teams that need a faster way to define a product and validate it with customers.

Sprint 1

In Lean UX, a sprint brings all key stakeholders together on a new project. The team works through ideas across a design week, meeting regularly in stand-ups to plan the next two days of work. Our sprints were fully virtual: we met on Discord for stand-ups and used Miro to test our assumptions and build our wireframes.

Design Week 0

We had no stand-ups in the first design week. Instead, we worked together to create a problem statement, list our assumptions, fill out the product backlog, create proto-personas, and set up the Sprint 1 backlog.

The goal for Week 0 was the problem statement. Stephanie elaborated on her app idea with the rest of the team, and we discussed and filled out the problem statement template together. We brainstormed assumptions about who our users would be and the problems they face when tracking their skincare and cycle. Since we had no actual stakeholders, the template also pushed us to consider the business goals and opportunities stakeholders would look for.

We concluded the meeting with a problem statement:

"The current state of skincare tracking products has focused primarily on external skin stressors such as sun exposure, environment, and stress. What existing products fail to address is hormonal factors, such as the menstrual cycle and how it affects skincare routines."


Next, we got all of our assumptions on the table. We filled out the assumptions template on Miro, splitting them into business assumptions (things we assume to be true in order to set strategy, make decisions, and plan the product) and user assumptions (what we assume to be true about our potential users).

Then we filled out the product backlog template to identify our hypothesis statements. The columns follow the form "we will achieve [outcome] if [this person] can achieve [goal] with [this feature]." We ended up with 11 hypotheses.

First product backlog.


After the backlog, we created our proto-personas. They're called proto-personas because they start from assumptions rather than research, and they get updated over the course of the sprints.

Through our later interviews, testing, and research, we determined that this product has three main types of users: beginner, intermediate, and expert. Creating these personas helped us understand who we were designing for and kept us from designing solely on assumptions.
Our personas are Claire Mueller, Dorothy Stewart, and Christina Ayer.

Claire Mueller persona.

Claire would like to track her menstrual cycle to build a personalized skincare routine. She needs an app that tracks her cycle and skincare together, so she can learn how her skin behaves during each phase and find better products for her skin type.

Dorothy Stewart persona.

Dorothyrecently started birth control and wants to track its symptoms. She noticed her skin getting drier and wants to understand how birth control affects it. She's already familiar with common skincare ingredients and side effects, and she wants to know which current products on the market best suit her.

Christina Ayer persona.

Christina is a skincare enthusiast who binges YouTube videos on the latest beauty products. She loves trying new products and changing up her routine, but needs somewhere to track it all and find personalized recommendations. Knowing her cycle matters to her too: she wants to test new products during the phase when her hormones and skin are most stable.



Lastly, we began our Sprint 1 backlog to figure out what we could accomplish in the sprint. Working from our assumptions, we discussed which features would be the most important, riskiest, and most valuable. We concluded that a product recommendation feature would take the highest level of effort.

Sprint Week 1

In our next design week, we held stand-ups every two days to plan the work ahead. Most importantly, this was the week we created our low-fidelity wireframe. We decided an app was the best way to address our problem statement, with the goal of validating (or invalidating) the concept. We built the wireframe in Miro, then recreated it in Figma as a clickable prototype. The prototype looked identical to the wireframe on purpose: we weren't focusing on visual design yet, just laying out the information and understanding how the pages would connect.

Low-fidelity wireframe snippet.

We also interviewed three participants, Emily, Neva, and Cady, to understand what people would want from a combined skincare and cycle-tracking app. We organized our questions into five groups: skincare routine, skincare products, website/app, menstrual cycle, and personal lifestyle. Each participant signed a consent form. After the interviews, we analyzed the responses as a team.


Sprint Week 2


We wanted to know whether people would find it helpful to know their cycle phase and what's happening with their skin every day. So we ran a lean experiment: for one week, we sent daily reminders to two participants telling them what phase they were in, what their skin was going through, and tips for caring for it. The "notifications" were sent as text messages.

1. Push Notifications

We were unsure whether daily notifications would be too intrusive, and whether users need phase reminders so often. To test this, we sent general reminders daily but limited phase notifications to every other day. The feedback: daily notifications ARE intrusive, but users liked certain reminders, especially routine and phase reminders. Our plan is notification settings users can toggle to customize which types they receive and how often.

2. Cycle Phase and Skin Complexion Tracking Features

One week wasn't enough data to fully predict a user's cycle. But by asking initial questions before the experiment, we could estimate each participant's phase and what information might help them. Both participants liked knowing their phase and wanted to learn more about it and its symptoms.

3. Info and Tips

We kept notifications short, so not all of them included tips; the ones that did were geared to the user's phase and the day of the week. Our first participant (matching the expert persona) didn't find the tips useful, since they covered things she already knew. The second participant found them helpful as reminders, and though she didn't save the specific tips we sent, she said the feature would be useful to her. We plan to add tags so users can filter which tips they see in the app and as notifications.

Overall Conclusion

We consider all three concepts validated. Based on the findings from this lean experiment, we kept the features and improved them using the feedback, with notification customization as the clearest next step.

Sprint 2

Sprint 2 followed the same structure as Sprint 1, with more focus on revalidating our original backlog, personas, and problem statement.

Design Week 0

We ran usability testing of the low-fidelity prototype with two participants, Emily and Sheila. We sent each a questionnaire and a document with instructions for navigating the prototype. Their responses fed directly into revalidating our problem statement:

Original Statement

"The current state of skincare tracking products has focused primarily on external skin stressors such as sun exposure, environment, and stress. What existing products fail to address is hormonal factors such as the menstrual cycle and how it affects skincare routines. Our product will address this gap by considering external skin stressors as well as the internal factor of the menstrual cycle. Our initial focus will be an app for women who wish to monitor and understand their menstrual cycle and the effect of hormonal changes on their skin."

Revalidated Statement:

"The current state of skincare tracking products has focused primarily on external skin stressors such as sun exposure, environment, and stress. Existing products fail to: educate users on different hormones and how they affect skin health, address factors such as the menstrual cycle, and list detailed descriptions and ingredients of products. Our product will address this gap by considering external skin stressors as well as the internal factor of the menstrual cycle, and by educating users on both. Our initial focus will be an app for women who wish to monitor and understand their menstrual cycle, the effect of hormonal changes on their skin, and their skincare routine."

Conclusion

Our goal with Iris was to help users manage their menstrual cycle while understanding how it affects their skin. Using feedback from potential users across interviews, questionnaires, a lean experiment, and three rounds of usability testing, we took Iris from assumptions to a high-fidelity prototype.
